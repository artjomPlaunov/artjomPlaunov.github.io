These are worked exercises from chapter 4 of "Practical Foundations for Programming Languages", by Bob Harper. I will just refer to this as [Book of Bob](https://www.cs.cmu.edu/~rwh/pfpl.html) from here on out, with a suitable link to the book. 


As per the book, the static phase is defined via a "statics" which is simply a set of rules that allows one to derive typing judgements stating that an expression is well formed of a certain type. [Book of Bob].

For context, this chapter presents the statics of a simple expression language **E**, and the rules defining the statics are listed (4.1a) to (4.1h) in the book, and all of them will come up in the proofs below. I will briefly describe the language: the syntax has two sorts, of Typ $$\tau$$ and Exp $$e$$. Typ ranges over the types num and str. Exp ranges over expressions of the form x, num[n], str[s], plus($$e_1$$;$$e_2$$), ..., let($$e_1$$;$$x.e_2$$). I elided some of the constructs in the ellipses, but this gives the basic idea. 

**Lemma 4.1 (Unicity of Typing)** For every typing context $$\Gamma$$ and expression $$e$$, there exists at most one $$\tau$$ such that $$\Gamma \vdash e : \tau$$.

The book says that this is proved using rule induction on rules (4.1), making use of the fact that variables have at most one type in any typing context. Seems like a reasonable thing to prove, perhaps I will come back and fill out this section with the wider "meaning" or point of this, for now I am just going along with the mechanics to learn the rules of the game.

**(4.1a)**


\begin{prooftree}
\AxiomC{}
\RightLabel{}
\UnaryInfC{$$\Gamma, x: \tau \vdash x : \tau$$}
\end{prooftree}

The assumption here, I believe, is that $$x$$ is fresh for $$\Gamma$$. This assumption can perhaps be more firmly justified via the definition of typing concepts presented in this chapter. In the definition of typing judgements, the typing context $$\Gamma$$ consists of a typing hypothesis for each variable in scope of the judgement. Hence there cannot be another typing judgement of the form $$x : \tau_1$$ in $$\Gamma$$, so our assumption checks out by definition. It immediately follows that $$x$$ can only be of at most one type, $$\tau$$. 

**(4.1b/c)**

\begin{prooftree}
\AxiomC{}
\RightLabel{}
\UnaryInfC{$$\Gamma \vdash str[s] : str$$}
\end{prooftree}

\begin{prooftree}
\AxiomC{}
\RightLabel{}
\UnaryInfC{$$\Gamma \vdash num[s] : num$$}
\end{prooftree}

In both cases, the typing context $$\Gamma$$ can only contain hypotheses relating to variables in the typing context. Since these axioms are the only place typing judgements about these two expressions are being introduced, and $$\Gamma$$ can't contain anything about these expression types, then it is clear we can only have 1 typing judgement about each respective expression (namely the one introduced in the axiom). 

**(4.1c)**

\begin{prooftree}
\AXC{$\Gamma \vdash e_1 : num$}
\AXC{$\Gamma \vdash e_2 : num$}
\BIC{$\Gamma \vdash plus(e_1,e_2) : num$}
\end{prooftree}
